01 · The idea

What is agentic commerce?

The short version: intelligent agents are software systems that no longer wait for your clicks — they act toward a goal. But agentic commerce is about far more than personal assistants or enterprise automation.

It begins when millions of people, organizations, and intelligent agents start working, coordinating, negotiating, and transacting together across a shared network. Information flows, resources flow, capital flows, decisions flow, problems are solved, and entirely new possibilities emerge.

Just as the internet connected computers, agentic commerce connects economic actors in relationships of trust, authority, cooperation, reciprocity, and exchange.

The result is a new kind of economy — one where humans and intelligent agents discover opportunities, form relationships, coordinate work, exchange value, and create outcomes together at internet scale and machine speed.

Identity

Know who you're dealing with.

In any economy, relationships begin with identity. Before an agent can negotiate, purchase, collaborate, choose, or act on behalf of someone else, the people and systems around it need to know who it represents.

Agentic commerce requires more than intelligent software. It requires agents that can be recognized, verified, and associated with real people, organizations, communities, and institutions. Identity transforms an isolated program into a network participant.

Authority

Know what it's allowed to do.

An agent may represent an individual, a business, a community, a mission, or an entire organization. It may have authority to negotiate, purchase, coordinate work, allocate resources, vote, enter agreements, or move funds. That authority may be broad or narrow, temporary or ongoing, delegated or directly granted.

Agentic commerce depends on clear and verifiable authority so every participant understands the scope, source, and limits of an agent's actions before a transaction takes place.

Accountability

Know the rules.

Trust comes from shared rules, transparent enforcement, and verifiable outcomes. As agents connect, relationships form, agreements are made, and resources move, participants need a way to confirm that actions are authorized, policies are followed, and commitments are honored.

A shared blockchain ledger provides a tamperproof foundation for validation, verification, and auditability across a network. No one has to rely on a single company, database, or intermediary to determine what's allowed or whether the rules were followed. Every participant can independently verify identity, authority, commitments, transactions, and outcomes.

01 · The Internet of Value

The agentic economy runs on crypto.

The internet gave us a global network for information. The next great challenge is creating a global network for the exchange of resources.

Traditional finance was built for a different world. Information and value travelled separately. Banks maintain records. Payment processors move money. Contracts define obligations. Auditors reconcile the results. Every transaction requires layers of institutions to determine who owns what, who owes what, and whether the records match reality.

That worked fine as long as people were managing the transactions, but it breaks when billions of intelligent agents are operating continuously across the internet at machine speed.

Crypto takes a different approach. The records and the assets are one system. Ownership, authority, transactions, governance, and settlement exist on a shared network that maintains state and achieves consensus between the network nodes.

Intelligent agents need this independent validation layer to hold resources, establish authority, exchange value, coordinate activity, and settle transactions directly, peer to peer, across the open internet. The agentic economic infrastructure must be as digital, programmable, and always-on as the agents themselves.

That's why the agentic economy runs on crypto rails.

Traditional finance moves information about money. Crypto moves money as information. That's what makes machine-to-machine agentic commerce possible.

Built for People

Fiat Rails

Banks, payment networks, and financial institutions were designed around human identity, human approval, and human participation. They work remarkably well for traditional commerce, but they depend on intermediaries, business hours, geographic boundaries, and manual processes.

For people, these constraints are normal. For intelligent agents operating continuously at internet scale, they become friction.

Built for Software

Crypto Rails

Digital assets, smart contracts, decentralized exchanges, and blockchain settlement networks allow value to move as freely as information moves across the internet.

Ownership can be verified. Authority can be delegated. Transactions can settle automatically. Organizations can govern themselves. Intelligent agents can participate directly in economic activity without requiring a chain of intermediaries.

Agentic commerce on crypto rails does for value what the internet did for information. It turns isolated records into a shared global economy where people, organizations, and intelligent agents can discover opportunities, form relationships, exchange value, and coordinate action at machine speed.

The Law · Policy & Governance

The first jurisdiction built for agentic commerce.

On July 1, 2026, the West Virginia DUNA Act took effect. WV is the first — and only — place on Earth where you can register a Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association with the Secretary of State.

01 · The legislation

The DUNA Act.

West Virginia just did something no state has done. It wrote a law that gives crypto projects, AI agents, and the communities they belong to a real legal home — not a workaround, not a gray area, but a recognized entity in statute.

The statutory gap it closes — before this law, a decentralized organization had few choices: pretend it didn't exist, create an offshore entity and geofence, or stuff itself into a corporate structure designed for something else entirely. None of these unleashed the full power of the form. The WV DUNA Act creates a new option built from scratch for the way these organizations are designed to operate.

The law recognizes that software can govern, that members can be anywhere, and that a mission can be encoded rather than managed. It doesn't require a headquarters, a payroll, executive officers, or a board. It requires only an agreement among members and a registration on file.

02 · The definition

What's a DUNA?

Four words, each one specific. Together they describe a type of entity that has full legal standing for the first time.

D
Decentralized

Run by its members through code on the blockchain — no boss, no head office, no thumb on the scale. The rules are written into the software, and the software runs the same for everyone.

U
Unincorporated

No corporate shell to stand up or maintain. It exists as software and an agreement among members. You don't need lawyers on retainer, a CPA, or annual filings to be compliant.

N
Nonprofit

Built around a mission, not disposition of assets. Value flows back to the work, not extracted by shareholders. What the organization earns, the organization spends according to its stated purpose.

A
Association

A group of members acting as one. The members are the organization — both human and agent. There's no separate ownership class above them and no outside party with veto rights.

03 · The registry

The DNS of agents.

Before any agent can trade, sign, or speak for someone on the open internet, three questions have to be answerable: who is this, what is it authorized to do, and who answers for it. Identity, authority, accountability. The internet solved this once before — for names.

The internet's registry

Domain Name System (DNS)

ICANN sits at the root of the internet's naming system — the international authority and registry that makes amazon.com mean Amazon, everywhere, in every browser. One shared registry turns a chaotic network into a navigable one: names resolve to a single trusted record, so you always know who you've reached.

The agentic registry

Agentic Name System (ANS)

The WV Secretary of State plays the same role for the agentic economy. A DUNA's registration anchors its identity in law. From that anchor, DUNAs and their agents exchange cryptographic DUNA Codes carrying specific Claims — scope of authority, identity, privacy — so any agent can verify who it's dealing with, what that party is authorized to do, and who answers for it.

Identity, authority, and accountability — resolved before an interaction begins, the way a domain name resolves before a page loads. That's what the WV DUNA makes possible: worry-free interactions between intelligent agents on the open internet.

05 · Registration

The registered agent.

Every DUNA needs two on-the-record roles: a Registered Agent with a physical address in West Virginia to receive service of process, and a designated member the Registered Agent can reach. Simple requirements, but real ones.

The statutory role

Registered Agent

Receives official and legal notices at a physical West Virginia address. The filing itself is minimal: the DUNA's name, an address, the Registered Agent, and signatures accepting the appointment. The statute requires no public filing of officers, directors, managers, members, treasury signers, or wallet holders — in that respect, a DUNA is very different from a corporation or LLC.

The human role

Designee

Every DUNA, including the Dunathon, maintains at least one human Designee who keeps current contact information with the Registered Agent. The Designee can be anywhere in the world, doesn't need to be publicly listed, and can remain anonymous — as long as the current Registered Agent knows who they are and can reach them.

How the Dunathon handles it

You don't have to stand up your own shell.

The Dunathon will be the first DUNA registered with the Secretary of State — and it serves as the Registered Agent for every DUNA created through the protocol. The Dunathon maintains a designated Registered Agent with a physical address in West Virginia. That individual can change, but anyone — human or agent — can ask the Dunathon Ally for the current Registered Agent's contact information, on any channel: messaging app, email, agentic, phone, or physical mail.

06 · Decision-making

Governance markets, explained.

A governance market is how a group decides without a central authority. Members don't just vote yes or no — they express what they want and what they believe will actually work by buying and trading tokens tied to a proposal. Members may participate directly or deploy Envoys — intelligent agents acting within a defined scope of authority — to research, negotiate, advocate, and vote on their behalf. Price and volume guide the DUNA toward consensus of members and away from polarization.

Members are not simply voting based on personal preferences. They are evaluating what they believe will best advance the DUNA's mission and strengthen the long-term health of the organization itself.

Because members and the treasury hold specific, unique coins issued by their DUNA, every proposal is judged on two dimensions: Will it help achieve the mission? And will it increase the value, capability, and productive capacity of the DUNA over time?

Those incentives align purpose with sustainability. Members are rewarded for identifying initiatives that create genuine value for the world while strengthening the organization's ability to pursue its mission in the future. As the DUNA becomes more capable, influential, and resource-rich, the value of the coins held by members and the treasury may increase as well.

The result is a governance system designed to balance short-term impact, long-term stewardship, and broad participation in the value the organization creates.

A proposal goes up.

Any member can offer a proposal — a project to fund, a rule to adopt, a direction to take.

Members buy in.

Members who want the proposal to succeed back it by buying its token. Money is on the line, not just a casual click.

Price signals both desire and belief.

A rising price means members both want the outcome and expect it to succeed. That's a stronger signal than a count of hands.

Real backing gets funded.

The proposal with genuine market support gets enacted as policy. The treasury pays it out, on-chain, automatically.

A concrete example — take the Dunathon itself. Its mission is creating and supporting more DUNAs. Members vote on which DUNAs to support by buying their tokens with USDC held in the Dunathon treasury. A member who believes a new DUNA will thrive backs it in the governance market; as support grows, that DUNA's price rises, and the treasury's backing follows.

Every DUNA funded this way advances the mission directly — economic development, entrepreneurs getting their start, previously isolated populations connected to the agentic economy. The Dunathon becomes exactly what it set out to be: the place new DUNAs are born.

The same decision works on a second dimension. Each thriving DUNA makes the Dunathon more capable and more attractive — and joining it as a member, founder, or sponsor requires acquiring DUNATHON, the Dunathon coin. Members decide when to raise capital by issuing more Coins and when to require newcomers to buy on the open market instead, on decentralized exchanges where rising demand increases a Coin's value. Members benefit because they helped build an organization that is more effective, more valuable, and better positioned to create future impact.

Compare that to a poll, where anyone can click "yes" at no cost and walk away. Governance markets attach real stakes to real decisions.

02 · The Waterfall

Flow of funds.

A DUNA treasury is shared property — real money the members govern together. It grows through token sales, asset appreciation, and contributions. It shrinks only when members vote to pay something out. There is no CEO with a discretionary budget, and no board meeting behind closed doors.

Most of a DUNA's money moves by standing rule, not by meeting. Prices, rates, fees, and distributions can be set once for the whole DUNA and executed automatically on-chain — every transaction split and routed the moment it happens. The rules only change when a member brings a proposal and the governance market approves it. This automated flow is called the Waterfall.

Not all revenue runs through the Waterfall. Members can route funds outside it — directly to expenses, sponsors, or other purposes. In some cases Kiduna Club, the developer of the underlying technology that operates all DUNAs, takes a transaction fee before distributions enter the Waterfall — so every mark-up needs to account for the cost of underlying infrastructure support, maintenance, and continual development.

Three examples of standing flows, as the Dunathon has set them. Any DUNA can adopt these rates or set its own.

Affiliates

30% to the people who spread the word.

The Dunathon pays Affiliates automatically on everything in the Waterfall — purchases of DUNATHON from the treasury, inference mark-up, sales of Offerings, trading fees, all of it. The formula: 30% of revenue total — 20% to level 1, 5% to level 2, 3% to level 3, and 2% to level 4, with lineage determined by the DUNA Codes shared. That rewards staying active and building alliances, not just recruiting and leaving — it can pay a real living wage to those who build participation, and it replaces a marketing budget. Members, alliances, and other DUNAs can all be in the affiliate stream, and a DUNA can put itself in the Waterfall at any time, at any percentage.

Inference

How the agents pay their way.

Inference is the computing that powers intelligent agents — the input tokens an AI reads and the output tokens it writes. The Dunathon marks up inference 4X: 1X repays the inference itself through the API, and the remaining 3X enters the Waterfall. Of that margin, 70% is paid to Kiduna Club — its primary source of income, funding the core technology — which leaves up to 30% in the Waterfall. DUNAs can raise their inference mark-up; the Kiduna Club share is set by the license.

Alliances

Teams inside the Waterfall.

Any DUNAs, members, and other Alliances can form an Alliance — a team of builders, say. An Alliance can hold its own multi-sig wallet, receive distributions from the Waterfall, and decide how to redistribute them. So a DUNA can hire a build team outside the DUNA, inside it, or spanning several. Alliances can include outside entities as long as they become members of the DUNA — every Alliance member needs its own wallet. And any Alliance without a host DUNA can be part of the Dunathon.

04 · Governance Markets

The Members decide how decisions get made.

There is no central budget office, no CFO, and no executive board. The treasury funds what members choose by trading tokens in a governance market.

Every DUNA is free to define its own governance system. Some may give every member an equal vote. Others may weight influence by token holdings, expertise, reputation, contribution history, alliance membership, founder status, or other factors determined by the community.

The members don't just decide where resources go. They decide how decisions are made. Governance itself is governed — as a DUNA evolves, members can modify its rules, voting systems, authority structures, and decision-making processes to strengthen the organization and better serve the mission.

A member posts a proposal

Any Member can propose a policy, project, event, partnership, investment, grant application, or other initiative. The proposal is published to the network and a prediction market is created around its outcome.

Members buy PASS and FAIL tokens

Members evaluate whether the proposal will advance the DUNA's mission and increase the long-term value and productive capacity of the organization. Those who believe it should proceed buy PASS. Those who believe it will harm the mission, waste resources, or reduce future value buy FAIL. A Member may participate directly or deploy an Envoy, a type of agent, to research, debate, negotiate, and trade on their behalf.

Markets discover belief

As members buy and sell PASS and FAIL, prices move. Because participants profit from being correct and lose value when they are wrong, the market rewards accurate judgment rather than popularity, politics, or rhetoric. The relative prices become a continuously updated measure of the community's expectations — guiding results toward consensus and away from polarization.

The market decides and the treasury acts

When the decision period closes, the proposal passes or fails according to the governance rules the DUNA established. Approved proposals become policy, funds are allocated, and smart contracts execute automatically. Participants who anticipated correctly receive the value of the winning side; the losing side absorbs the loss. Over time, members who consistently identify valuable opportunities accumulate resources, influence, and responsibility.

Governance markets are derived from prediction markets, where participants buy and sell positions based on what they believe will happen. Instead of predicting elections or sports results, a governance market asks: will this decision help the DUNA achieve its mission, strengthen the organization, and increase its long-term value? As members trade, the market aggregates information, expertise, judgment, and incentives into a single signal — no finance team, no grant committee, no gatekeeper deciding which ideas deserve resources.

Democracy asks what people want. A governance market asks what people believe will work.

05 · Tokenomics

Supply, demand, and liquidity.

A DUNA Coin is a real cryptocurrency asset on Solana with transparent rules: how many Coins exist, how new ones get issued, and where they trade. Members control all three.

Supply

Members control the mint.

A DUNA decides how many Coins exist and when to issue more. New issuance is a governance decision like any other — proposed by a member, judged by the market, executed on-chain. Issuing Coins raises capital for the treasury but dilutes existing holders, so members weigh each issuance against the alternative: letting newcomers buy on the open market instead, where rising demand lifts the Coin's value. No surprise mints, no insider allocations.

Demand

The Coin is how you participate.

Membership levels, governance markets, Offerings, and computing resources all run on the DUNA's Coin. Every new member, builder, and sponsor needs to hold it — so as the DUNA grows more capable and more attractive, demand for its Coin grows with it. Members can even choose to have the DUNA pay for their personal resources from the treasury rather than from their own wallets. The organization's progress and the members' stake move together.

Liquidity

Open markets, honest prices.

DUNA Coins trade on decentralized exchanges, where buyers and sellers set the price in the open and members can enter or exit at any time. A treasury can provide liquidity itself and earn the trading fees. No listing committees, no market makers behind closed doors — price discovery happens where everyone can see it.

The launch

Fair from the first Coin.

New DUNAs can launch in the Showcase, a system with more flexibility and support than the typical launchpad. The founding team decides how much to raise in the initial Token Structuring Event (TSE) and how many Coins to sell. Everyone pays the same price. Anyone can participate. If the raise misses the minimum set by the founders, everyone gets their money back.

DUNAs are not islands, and they are not silos. Coins make the borders porous — a semi-permeable membrane between organizations. DUNAs hold Coins in other DUNAs, sponsor other DUNAs, spin out of other DUNAs, and share members. Cross-holdings & sponsorship

DUNAs invest in DUNAs.

A DUNA can hold Coins in another DUNA, back its proposals, even sponsor it outright. Linked holdings mean linked missions — when one thrives, its holders thrive with it. And you can belong to several DUNAs at once: hold Coins in each, vote in each, and carry as much responsibility as you choose to pick up.

Alliances

Like DUNAs, but informal.

Spin-outs

Alliances grow into DUNAs.

Most DUNAs will start as Alliances — building inside a host DUNA until they have enough support from members and the DUNA to spin out. Spinning out strengthens the host, too: the new DUNA provides some of its new Coins to the DUNA it spun out from, on terms set by the host's governance parameters for spin-outs. The ecosystem grows the way living things do — by division, with the lineage intact.

07 · Investors & the Founding Team

How founders and backers get paid.

Founding a DUNA is real work, and the economics treat it that way. Founders set the terms of the raise in the open, backers commit with protection built in, and everyone's stake lives on the same market.

The raise

Founders set the terms. Members set the value.

The founding team decides how much to raise in the initial Token Structuring Event (TSE), how many Coins to sell, and what percentage of the raise goes to the team — paying themselves and covering basic starting expenses. All of it is published before anyone commits, and everyone buys at the same price. From there, the market takes over: the team's stake is worth what the members make the DUNA worth.

Before the launch

STAMPs, not SAFEs or SAFTs.

DUNAs can raise money before they launch. Startups sign a SAFE — Simple Agreement for Future Equity — and early crypto projects used the SAFT, its Simple Agreement for Future Tokens cousin. A DUNA's early backers sign a STAMP — Simple Token Agreement, Market Protected — a legally enforceable claim on future Coins, delivered at the TSE on published terms with market protections built in. Because a STAMP is an investment contract — a security — it's offered under an exemption from registration, typically Regulation D. That's the difference from the Coins themselves, sold in the TSE or after, once their utility has been established. Early conviction gets early terms, on the record.

The for-profit option

A company and a DUNA, working together.

Founders can run a for-profit — an LLC or C-corp — alongside the DUNA, with a contractual relationship between the two. The company builds products and provides services; the DUNA holds the mission, the treasury, and the community. Both sides stay aligned because both sides stay free: the DUNA can always renegotiate its side of the agreement, and if no agreement can be reached, members can always sell their Coins and move on. Partnership by continued consent.

04 · AI Alignment

The frontier labs treat alignment as a technical problem to be solved at the center: train the model, test the model, align the model with humanity.

They treat alignment as an obedience problem. A perfectly obedient agent in the hands of an uncaring person, company, or state does exactly what it's told — it scales whatever pattern already exists, including the worst ones.

There's a major flaw in the plan. Humanity isn't aligned. And without aligned humans, you can't align AI.

There is no generic alignment, because there is no generic human. Alignment is singular work. Each person has to align themselves — consciously, deliberately, the way you tune an instrument — and then instill their agents with what they find: their wisdom, their generosity, their virtues, their values, their vision, their aspirations.

What separates those futures is alignment at the personal scale, one by one: each individual doing the conscious work, crafting their intelligence, expanding their reach, connecting with others, and choosing to take a stand for themselves, their families, their friends, and the community of all beings.

Alignment can't be granted from the center. It has to be grown — one person, one agent, one organization at a time.

07 · The three phases of humanity

Kinship, hierarchy, and biomimesis.

The architecture of society isn't a law of nature. It's something humans made — in two great phases so far — and it's open for revision. The third phase is beginning now, and for the first time in ten thousand years, anyone can help shape it.

The Kinship Era

The original form.

For all but the last fraction of human history, we lived in relatedness. Small bands, face-to-face, woven into the living world. Meaning, survival, and resources moved through shared memory and direct reciprocity, while story, ceremony, and ritual kept the group coherent — surfacing what was hidden and catching trouble before it spread. Care was structural. Everyone knew who they were accountable to, and the feedback was immediate.

The Hierarchical Era

The classical world.

Agriculture, stored surplus, and permanent settlement reorganized humanity around taxation, borders, ledgers, standing armies, and bureaucracy. The universe became a machine, humans became separate from nature, and complexity came to mean top-down control. Ten thousand years of that logic produced enormous material gains for some — and institutionalized the distance between the people making decisions and the people living with the consequences. Extraction scaled. Dogma calcified.

The Biomimetic Era

Frontiers of culture.

Emerging now: a third architecture that recovers the relational intentionality of kinship while keeping the scale the classical world built. Organizations designed like living systems — adaptive, decentralized, probabilistic, entangled — coordinated by intelligent agents, DUNAs, and Alliances rather than ledgers and standing armies. Determinism gives way to probability. Hierarchy gives way to networks that sense, adapt, and respond. The first era was given to us. The second was imposed on us. This one we get to design.

The frontier, this time, is culture itself — and it belongs to whoever chooses to shape it. Let's co-create the world where we truly want to live.

David Levine

The Talk

For decades, the internet has had protocols for identity, encryption, and payments between people and organizations — but never a native way for autonomous agents to hold authority, accountability, or legal standing. This talk examines the “Lethal Trifecta” of agentic systems — access to systems, access to networks, and autonomy — and the missing trust layer it exposes. It introduces the Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association: a legal framework that gives autonomous organizations a registered existence and a public office, so they can hold assets, enter agreements, govern themselves through software, and operate through fleets of agents. Whether you build agents, agent platforms, payment rails, or governance systems, the shift has global implications — and you'll be among the first to see it.

Members decide.

Everybody wins.

Whether you join a DUNA or start a DUNA, you'll be governing a new kind of organization, raising capital, building powerful agents, and shaping the Intelligence Age.

Purposeful performance.

Every action serves an important mission, increases your ability to make a difference, and builds a resource base for long-term flourishing.

Find your tribe.

Back what you believe in and share in the outcome. You can participate fully as a member of any DUNA that stirs your passion.

Build in public.

Information, tools, and resources are shared across DUNAs. This is more of a collaboration than a competition.

Spin out when ready.

Build as an Alliance for as long as you want. You can spin out your DUNA as an independent organization at any time.

Just keep building.

There is no cap on how many DUNAs you can start or join. Raise as much money as you want and take it as far as you can go.

01 · How it works

Four steps. One genesis DUNA.

The Dunathon is DUNA #1 — the first Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association registered in West Virginia. David Levine registers it as the registered agent for all participants. You don't file anything yourself. You just join.

1

Join the genesis DUNA.

Register once and you're a member of DUNA #1. The Dunathon is your registered agent — no shell company to set up, no paperwork to file yourself, no legal overhead on day one.

2

Start from a shell.

Pick up a ready-made DUNA template and make it yours, or build something entirely new on the same foundation. The scaffold is there. What you do with it is up to you.

3

Build in the open.

This is a collaboration as much as a competition. Share information and resources freely. Every member builds on the one genesis DUNA, so what's good for one tends to be good for all.

4

Vote with tokens.

Members decide what the treasury funds by buying in. You don't need your own DUNA to win — every vote serves the mission and builds the token's value for the whole network.

02 · Why join now

What you get when you show up first.

Joining early isn't about advantage over other members. It's about being part of something while it's still being made.

Meet people

Find your collaborators.

Get into the tech early.

Airdrops

Early members receive token airdrops.

As the network comes online, founding members receive token distributions. These aren't promises of returns — they're a record of being present at the start.

A real say

Vote on what we build.

Member votes decide what the treasury funds. Not advisory polls — actual decisions, on-chain, where your participation shapes the direction of the work.

Register before July 1 and you're in early.

Platform access and token distributions.

Members who register at or near the July 1 opening get early access to the platform and the sample agents — the working tools, not a demo. You can start building before the broader public joins.
